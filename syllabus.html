DESCRIPTION

This is a studio based course that introduces students to computer programming with an emphasis on creative design and data visualization. No previous programming experience is required. Students will learn techniques to translate raw data into compelling audio and visual projects. Through readings and discussions, the course will also unpack the roles data plays in our lives. Composing a series of projects throughout the semester, students will experiment and develop their own strategies to creatively write code. Students will complete the class with technical skills that will lay the foundation for more advanced programming, scripting, and web development.

There are no prerequisites for this course.

**Having your own computer is a requirement for this class**

STUDENT RESPNSIBILITIES AND REQUIREMENTS

Students are expected to be prepared at the start of each class with their laptops ready, with the required software installed, with any readings completed, and amterials to take notes. Class projects offer opportunities to guide content based upon individual interests. Students are invited and encouraged to interset their research interests with the course work. This class will focus on using code creatively and requires an openness and enthusiasm for artistic thinking.

COURSE GOALS

The goal of this course is to establish a foundational understanding of digital and code environments as art making tools. Through lectures, screenings, and workshops, students will learn about the social, political, and personal relationships these technologies form in our everyday lives. Students will also form a critical foundation through investigations into theories and histories.

Basic understanding of how computers, networks, and code work

A general understanding of how to read and write new media using various techniques such as HTML, CSS, JavaScript, Generative systems, and Art Games.

Broad understanding of the political, historical, social, and creative implications of living in an interconnected virtual, electronic world. How these systems of code change the way we relate to media, and what it means to exist between the virtual and the physical.

Leave this course with a beginner to intermediate level skill set in order to take additional new media classes.

Students will learn to write code that:

  • Generates visual and audio compositions
  • Uses input from the computer keyboard and mouse
  • Uses information from datasets
  • Read and learn from others' code
  • Troubleshoot issues and learn their causes

PROJECTS

A series of projects will be announced throughout the semester supporting the course objectives. To succeed at these projects and to achieve the most from participating in the course, students must invest in the technical, conceptual, and aesthetic compnents of the work. Skills required for these projects will be covered through lessons, workshops, and homework assignments. Students are strongly encouraged to creatively experiment and to go above and beyond the minimum requirements for projects.

CLASS MATERIALS

Various articles and excerpts from journals, websites, and books covering topics such as critical theory, experimental new media art, data visualization, and computer graphics will be handed out in class as reading assignments. There are no required books you need to purchase for this class.

EVALUATION

Students must challenge themselves to grow based upon thier previous skills and experience. Students should set goals that are realistic and push themselves beyond their previous work. Projects that demonstrate attention to detail, thoughtful choices, and careful craft will be evaluated much higher than overly ambitious work that remains incomplete or dysfunctional. Classmates are expected to contribute to a supportive and helpful classroom environment.

GRADING

Grading is based on expectations of class participation, reading responses, and quality of work created.

10% In Class Participation: Class participation includes lab time, workshops, critiques, discussions, and an overall active engagement in class.

10% Homework: There will also be a few short homework exercises for you to complete throughout the semester. Information for each homework assignment will be given in class and listed on the class schedule.

40% Assignments: These assignments will be discussed in class and will be posted on the course website with adequate time for completion.

40% Final: The final project will consist of an artistic work or installation of your choosing utilizing the skills obtained in lab exercises and inspired by course content. Please see the homework and assignments section for details.

Assignments submitted after the due date will be penalized one letter grade. No assignments will be accepted for credit later than one week beyond the due date.

  • One-Three absences – no penalty except for loss of instruction
  • Four absences – grade lowered one letter grade
